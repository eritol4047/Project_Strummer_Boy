Acoustic Guitar

Is a guitar that has a Sound Hole and has strings tied to the bridge. This type of guitar is more natural sounding.

Beat

It is the unit of musical time. It is much like a heartbeat or a pulse, in that it is measured within a period of time.

Body

This is where you strum and pluck the strings. On this part of the guitar, there is a hole on acoustic guitar where you strum the strings. On Electric guitars, there is no hole, you just strum or pluck the string at the center between where the strings are tied and the base of the neck.

Bridge

This is a device that supports the strings and transmits the vibration. It’s on the body side of the guitar and it is where the strings are tied in place. On an electric guitar, the strings are tied past the bridge instead.

Chords

These refer to how one or more fingers are placed on the Neck of the guitar but are always strummed in a pattern. They have one or more fingers placed on different frets on the Neck. For example, a note might be the lower E string on the second fret. Plucked it’s a note, but strummed with other fingers, it becomes a chord.

Cutaway

This is an indentation on the top body of a guitar to allow for easier access to the highest frets on a guitar. These aren’t important for guitars to have, but it is helpful.

Electric Guitar

This is a guitar that has Pickups in place of a Sound Hole and has knobs, a pickup selector, and an output jack. This type of guitar paired with a speaker is what can give a huge variety of sounds.

Frets

These are the spaces between the vertical lines when holding the guitar on your lap. Some of these have dots on them indicating the number fret it is.

Head

Refers to the top of the guitar where you can turn the knobs.

Knobs

On an electric guitar, these are volume/tone adjusters where you can remotely control the volume on the guitar itself.

Nut

This is a notched piece of plastic or bone between the neck and head on a guitar. The notches are the strings sit in order to keep them aligned across the neck while pulled onto the pegs above.

Neck

This is the main part of the guitar where your off hand goes. This is where you place your fingers along the center of each fret. Most are right-handed, meaning you will place your left hand, other guitars have you place your right hand on this part of the guitar.

Notes

A note simply refers to the fret where you place your finger to make the sound. For example, A1 refers to the first fret on the string second from the top of the guitar, or second from the lower E string depending on how the person is referring to it. Either way it’s the same note.

Output Jack

This is a plug that you use to plug a guitar into a speaker for a louder, or even different, sound. These are on all electric guitars but are less common on acoustic guitars.

Pick

This is a tool that is shaped in a triangle that is used to pluck or strum the strings of a guitar.

Pick Guard

This is the big space on a guitar that goes on top of the wood underneath the Sound Hole. This is to preserve the wood from scratches that could be made when strumming or plucking with a pick.

Pickup Selector Switch

On Electric guitars, these are small switches used for getting a different sound out of a guitar. They typically have 3 to 5 modes depending on the model.

Pickups

These are devices on electric guitars that pick up the sound, are embedded into the body and come in two versions. One with six magnetic bars, one for each string and the other with a single magnet across metal rods.

Pluck

The action of pulling a string on the body to note a sound.

Rythm

The systematic arrangement of musical sounds. This is performed with notes hitting in a specific pattern.

Sound Hole

This is the hollowed-out hole on the body of the guitar. Contrary to popular belief, it doesn’t produce sound, but is designed to amplify sound the sound without a speaker.

Strings

These are the vibrating elements that make the sound on a guitar. Some guitars have more than 6 strings, and others have less. This is what differentiates string instruments from the other types of instruments. The most common guitars have 6 strings, but there are guitars out there with 4, 7, and even 12 strings.

Strum

The action of moving a finger, thumb or pick across a guitar pulling multiple strings to create a harmonious sound of strings.

Tempo

This is the pace in which a song is to be played. This can vary with performance, but all instruments within the same performance follow a particular pace. Tempo in Italian translates to time. In music, we simply refer to it being slow, quick or moderate.

Time Signature

This is the two numbers at the beginning of a music sheet that indicates two things; how many beats in a measure, and the pace or how many beats is that whole meausure. A 4⁄4 measure indicates that the 4 on top is the beats per measure and the bottom 4 is the basic note measurement within that measure. It gets confusing at first, but just count every 4 beats as a measure, then play the notes in any rythm while counting to 4 and it'll start to become clear.

Tuning Pegs

Refers to the six points on each side of the head. These are used for tuning the strings. The tighter you turn a peg, the higher the sound on the string when pulled. The looser the string, the lower the sound. Make sure you start tuning here.
